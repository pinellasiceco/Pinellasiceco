Know Your Market
Pinellas County — who you’re selling to
▼
The Pinellas Restaurant Owner

Pinellas County’s median age is 49 — older than the state average. The restaurant owners you’ll meet are predominantly established operators. Many have owned their business for 10–20 years. They’ve been pitched by dozens of vendors and have a sharp instinct for salespeople. They buy from people they trust, not from people who pitch them.

The county’s economy is anchored in tourism and hospitality. Clearwater Beach, St. Pete Beach, and downtown St. Pete generate millions of visitor days per year. High foot traffic means high ice demand and machines that run harder and get dirtier faster. Florida heat and humidity mean bacteria and scale grow faster here than almost anywhere else in the country.

⚡ Seasonal insight: June–September, Pinellas waterfront restaurants run machines at 90%+ capacity. That’s when neglected machines fail and inspections spike. The best window to close these accounts is March–May, before summer creates urgency. Sell prevention before the pain arrives.
Cultural Landscape — Who You’ll Meet

Pinellas is 71% White, 11% Hispanic, 9% Black. The adjacent Hillsborough market is 25% Hispanic with the highest concentration of Cuban and Puerto Rican ownership in the region. As your territory grows, these segments grow.

Cuban and Hispanic-owned restaurants are among your best prospects — family-operated, independent, owner usually present, community-networked. The concept of confianza (earned trust) governs decision-making. Do not rush. Do not use pressure. Ask about their restaurant before you mention your service. A warm referral from a trusted community contact will outperform 20 cold walk-ins in this segment.

Long-established independents (10+ years) are your most stable recurring clients. They’ve seen operators lose their license. They care about compliance. They respond to expertise and reliability over price. Earn trust once and they become advocates.

Newer independents (under 5 years) are more price-sensitive but also more open. They haven’t built their full vendor roster and are often genuinely unaware of what professional ice machine maintenance looks like.

Car to close — every step

  4. 4
    Get out of the car.

Step 1 — At the Host Stand

Walk in with quiet confidence. You look like a technician making a courtesy stop — not a salesperson. That distinction matters more than you think in a market full of vendors owners have learned to avoid.

At host stand
"Hi, I’m looking for the owner or general manager — is [name] available?"

If they ask what it’s about: "I’m a commercial ice machine sanitation tech — I’m in the area and wanted to stop by for two minutes."

Never say "sales call." Never say "tell you about our services."

Step 2 — The Opening Sandler

The first sentence determines everything. Sandler principle: open with their world, not yours. Create a question in their mind before you answer anything.

The Sandler opener
"I only need two minutes. Can I ask you one question? When was the last time someone actually opened your ice machine and documented what they found?"

This is a pain funnel entry. They either say "recently" — ask who and how often, which reveals the gap — or they say "I’m not sure," which means you’ve already found the pain.

Listen. Do not fill silence. What they say next tells you more about how to close them than anything you’ll say in response.

Step 3 — The Machine Offer Challenger
The pivot
"Mind if I take a look right now? Two minutes. I’ll tell you exactly what an inspector would flag — no charge, no obligation."

When you open the machine, say nothing for a moment. Let them look. If there’s scale or biofilm visible, let the silence work. You don’t need to editorialize what they can see with their own eyes.

Step 4 — ATP Demo (see Section 02)

Hand them the meter. Say nothing while it reads. The number closes.

Step 5 — The Offer SPIN

After the reading, use SPIN’s Implication + Need-Payoff before presenting price:

Implication (SPIN)
"If an inspector ran that same test during a surprise visit, what would that mean for your license?"

Let them answer. Then:

Need-Payoff (SPIN)
"If I could give you a compliance report showing a 90%+ reduction in that reading — documented, dated, available to show any inspector — how would that change things?"

Then:

The offer
"Here’s what I do. A $99 first visit — I assess the machine, run ATP tests before and after, do a service and sanitize, and leave you a compliance report. If the machine needs a full deep clean, that’s $349 total and your $99 is credited. After that, 60-day maintenance at $149/month — six visits a year, two Everpure filter replacements included. No contract."

Stop. Wait for them to respond.

When the Owner Isn’t There
Leave-behind handoff
"Can you make sure [name] sees this? Tell them the ice machine tech stopped by."

A compliance report on a manager’s desk has a shelf life. A business card gets thrown away.

The ATP Pitch Protocol
Your single most powerful selling tool
▼
Why This Closes Challenger

The ATP test is a textbook Challenger Sale. You’re showing them something true about their own business they couldn’t see. Numbers remove opinion from the conversation. An owner can argue with your words. They cannot argue with 847 RLU on a meter they’re holding in their own hands.

⚠ Never talk while the meter is reading. The silence is intentional. The moment they see the number is your highest-leverage selling moment. Don’t dilute it with words.
Pre-Clean Swab Locations — Maximum Contamination
  1. 1
    Water trough interior — biofilm accumulates here constantly. Wet, dark, never cleaned by staff. Almost always the highest reading. Z-pattern, firm pressure.
  2. 2
    Underside of water curtain — dark, permanently wet, invisible to staff. Biofilm starts here. Swab the underside edge.
  3. 3
    Ice scoop handle — touched hundreds of times daily. Cross-contamination. Looks clean. Reads high.
  4. 4
    Inside bin near drain — ice runoff pools. Always elevated.
  5. 5
    Float valve housing — scale and organic material concentrate here. Rarely cleaned.
The Pitch Move — Execute Exactly
  1. 1
    Open the front panel.
  2. 2
    Say nothing. Let them look first.
  3. 3
    Pull out Ultrasnap. Snap tube. "Let me show you something."
  4. 4
    Swab trough, Z-pattern, firm pressure. About 4 seconds.
  5. 5
    Insert swab into Ensure v2. Hand the meter to the owner. Say nothing.
  6. 6
    15 seconds. Let them watch the countdown. Let them read the number themselves.
  7. 7
    Pause 2 full seconds after they read it. Then: "Anything over 30 is a potential health inspection citation. Pass is 10 or below."

Objection Handler
Every pushback, exact response
▼
The Core Principle Sandler

An objection is not a no. It’s a request for more information, delivered poorly. Every objection has a root fear. Address the fear, not the surface statement. Sandler A-R-C: Acknowledge → Reframe → Close. Never argue. Never defend. Redirect.

Common Objections
🔴 "We clean it ourselves."
Root fear: Being told they’re doing something wrong. Ego investment.

"I’m sure you do — running the clean cycle and wiping down the exterior is exactly the right habit. What I do is different. I open the water system, pull the components, descale the evaporator, and test the contamination levels with a meter before and after. That’s the part inspectors actually look at — and you can’t get to it without disassembly." Then: "Can I run a 30-second test right now? You’ll see exactly where you stand — no charge."
✓ The meter settles it without argument.
🔴 "We already have someone."
Root fear: Switching vendors feels like work. Stability preference.

"Good — how often do they come out? And do they give you a written report with ATP readings you can show an inspector?" Pause. If no: "That’s the gap. Anyone can run a cleaning service. We prove it was done to a standard that protects your license." If yes: "Excellent. Would you be open to a $99 comparison visit just to benchmark? A second set of eyes on a food safety item never hurts."
✓ "Most of our clients had someone before us. The difference is the documentation."
🔴 "That’s too expensive / we can’t afford it."
Root fear: Adding a line item. Budget pressure is real in restaurants.

"Most services around here charge $250 to $300 for a single visit. Crystal Ice is $295. We’re $149 a month — six visits a year, every 60 days, two Everpure filter replacements included. More service for less than one competitor visit." Then: "What would a health inspection citation cost you — in fines, reinspection fees, and the Yelp review that follows?"
✓ "$99 intro, no commitment. One visit to see what the documentation looks like."
🔴 "My landlord handles that."
Root fear: Not my problem to solve.

"Do they have documentation of the last service — a compliance report you could show an inspector?" (Almost always: no.) "The challenge is that when the inspector writes a citation, it goes on your license, not your landlord’s. The responsibility is yours as the licensee." Offer to handle the service directly and send the documentation to building management. You’re solving their problem, not creating one.
✓ "I handle it, you hand the report to your landlord. Everybody’s covered."
🔴 "Let me think about it."
Root fear: Missing information — or no pain felt yet. Sandler: no pain, no sale.

Never hand a card and leave. "Of course — what specifically would be most helpful to know before you decide?" Listen. If they can’t articulate it, the real objection hasn’t surfaced. "Most people want to see the documentation before committing. Can I show you a before-and-after report from a job I did nearby this week?"
✓ "I’m back in this area Thursday. Can I stop by when [owner] is in?" Always leave with a defined next step.
🔴 "We just passed our last inspection."
Root fear: If it ain’t broke, don’t fix it.

"That’s great — when was it?" Then: "Inspectors don’t always swab inside the unit — they’re often checking paperwork and visible surfaces. Would you like to see what the water contact surfaces look like right now?" The ATP demo still closes this. A passing inspection and a clean machine are two different things.
✓ "Our goal isn’t just passing. It’s never having that conversation with an inspector at all."
🔴 "Your competitor is cheaper."
Root fear: Overpaying. Comparison shopping.

"That’s possible. Do they provide ATP documentation with before-and-after readings? And a compliance report for your records?" Most cheaper services don’t. "The price difference is the documentation." Then give context: "A single professional cleaning in this market typically runs $250–300. Our monthly is $149, and we’re there 12 times a year, not twice."
✓ "I’m not the cheapest. I’m the one you call when an inspector walks in."
🔴 "I need to talk to my partner / corporate."
Respect this one. Don’t push a close they don’t have authority to make.

"Totally understand — who else is involved in this decision?" Get the name. "What would be most helpful to leave with you for that conversation?" Leave excellent materials. "Would it be easier if I came back when [name] is here, or would it help to send information directly to them?" Never leave without a defined next step.
✓ Get a specific date, name, or contact method. A vague "they’ll call you" is a dead end.
🟢 “What’s actually included in the $149/month?”
Buying signal. Answer completely and confidently.

“Every 60 days I come in, open the water system, descale the evaporator, sanitize all internal surfaces, run ATP tests before and after, and leave you a compliance report. Twice a year I also replace your Everpure Insurice filter — that’s the filter on your water line that prevents scale on the evaporator and extends machine life. Most service companies charge extra for filter changes or don’t include them. It’s built into your $149. Six visits a year, two filter replacements, ATP documentation every visit.”
✓ This answer closes more often than not. Have your pricing card ready.

The Close
Read the room and seal the deal
▼
The Core Rule

The close is the natural conclusion to a conversation where you’ve established real value. If someone doesn’t buy, you either didn’t establish enough value or you pitched the wrong person at the wrong time.

Never close with a question that can be answered "no." Close with a question that assumes yes and asks for a detail — a date, a preference, a name.
Primary Closes
The Assumptive Close
Use when: strong buying signals, nodding, follow-up questions, concern about the ATP reading
Script
"Let me get you set up. What day works best for the first service — I typically do Tuesday and Thursday in this area."
The Risk-Removal Close
Use when: interested but hesitant, needs a low-commitment entry point
Script
"Let’s start with the $99 intro visit. I assess the machine, do a service and sanitize, and run before-and-after ATP tests — all documented in a compliance report. If the machine needs a full deep clean, that’s $349 total and the $99 is credited. After that, 60-day maintenance at $149/month with two Everpure filter replacements per year included. No contract at any point."
The Evidence Close
Use when: skeptic, wants proof before any commitment
Script
"Let me show you a before-and-after report from a service I did this week. If the results look like something you’d want for your own kitchen, we schedule a visit."
The Graceful Exit (Firm No)
Use when: firm no after 2+ attempts — don’t push further
Script
"Completely understand. Can I leave this with you? If anything changes — new inspection, new season, your current service misses a visit — I’m easy to reach. Number’s on there."

Leave gracefully. Log it. Set a 60-day follow-up. The ones who say no today remember who left with class.

The Follow-Up Sequence
Where most commercial service deals are won
▼
The Core Truth

80% of commercial service sales require 5 or more touchpoints. Most vendors give up after 2. Your follow-up discipline is a competitive advantage in itself. The goal of each touchpoint is never to push a close — it’s to add value and be there when something changes. Something always changes.

Standard Follow-Up Sequence
0
Same Day — Log Everything
Log in app: owner name, reaction, specific objection, machine brand, next action. Set Day 7 follow-up before you leave the parking lot.
7
Day 7 — The Phone Call
Call directly. If voicemail: "Hi, this is [name] from Pinellas Ice Co — I stopped by last week and looked at your ice machine. I have a few notes I wanted to share. Two minutes when you get a chance — [number]." Don’t pitch in the voicemail. Create curiosity.
14
Day 14 — The Walk-Back
Return in person if in the area. Brief, not a full visit. "I was in the neighborhood and wanted to drop something off for [owner]." Leave a before-and-after comparison from a recent nearby job. Presence, not pressure.
30
Day 30 — The Intelligence Drop
Text or call with genuine value: "Hey, this is [name] from Pinellas Ice Co. FL DBPR is stepping up inspections in Pinellas this month — wanted to give you a heads up. Happy to get you documented if it would help." Real intel from your Monday briefing email. Real information creates real conversations.
60
Day 60 — Qualify or Close the Loop
One more call or walk-in. If they’re not interested after 5 touchpoints, close gracefully: "I’ve been in a few times and I know the timing hasn’t been right. I’ll take you off my active list — but keep my number. If anything changes with inspections or your current service, I’m a call away."
The Referral Ask — Highest ROI Activity

After every completed service, before you leave:

The referral ask
"Do you know any other restaurant owners in the area who might want to see a report like this? I’ll give them the same $99 intro, and I’ll take care of you if they sign up monthly."

Ask in person, every time. One warm referral in Cuban and Caribbean owner networks is worth 15 cold walk-ins. These communities talk to each other constantly.

Field Mindset
What separates the top 10% from everyone else
▼
The Three Rules
  1. 1
    Volume solves everything. A decent pitch on 20 walk-ins closes more than a perfect pitch on 3. The pitch sharpens through use, not through thinking about it.
  2. 2
    You are providing a genuine service. Restaurants with dirty ice machines are a liability risk to their owners and a health risk to customers. Walk in like someone who has something valuable to offer — because you do.
  3. 3
    The no is free. Every walk-in costs 10 minutes. A no costs nothing. The fear of rejection is the only thing between you and your goal. Feel it and go in anyway.
Call Reluctance — The Real Obstacle

The Post-Walk-In Debrief — 90 Seconds, Every Time

Do this immediately after every walk-in — close or no close. Sitting in the car before you drive away. This is how you compound learning faster than any competitor.

5 Questions. Answer out loud.
  1. 1
    What happened? Closed, didn’t close, owner wasn’t there, got the ATP demo in, didn’t get past the host stand. One sentence.
  2. 2
    What objection did they give? Not what they said — what was the real fear underneath it? "We already have someone" = fear of switching hassle. "Can’t afford it" = fear of new line item. Name the actual fear.
  3. 3
    What moment did I lose them? Or if you closed: what moment turned it? Be specific. "When I said $149 they went quiet" or "When they saw the ATP number they leaned in."
  4. 4
    What would I do differently? One thing only. Not a list. One specific change for the next walk-in.
  5. 5
    Log it. Open the app. Note the objection, the outcome, and the one change. Set the follow-up. Drive away.
After 20 walk-ins with this debrief you will know more about how Pinellas restaurant owners buy than anyone who’s been doing this for years without reflecting. The reps compound. The debrief is what turns reps into skill.
